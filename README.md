Project Overview: This dashboard delivers a comprehensive view of workforce health for a mid-sized organization of 1,470 employees, tracking attrition patterns, demographic distribution, and education-linked talent loss. Built on the IBM HR Analytics dataset, it enables HR leaders and business partners to move from reactive headcount management to proactive retention strategy — answering not just how many people left, but who left, from where, and at what age.

The Business Story This Dashboard Tells: 

A. The organization is experiencing a 16% attrition rate — with 237 employees lost against an active workforce of 1,233. On the surface, that number is manageable. But the dashboard reveals a far more nuanced story beneath it.

B. The R&D department is bleeding talent. With 133 attritions — over 56% of total employee exits — R&D is carrying a disproportionate share of the organization's talent loss. Sales follows at 92, while HR at 12 is relatively stable. This signals that the retention problem is not company-wide — it is concentrated, and that concentration demands targeted intervention.

C. The 25–34 age band is the highest-risk segment. The bottom donut section shows 112 attritions in this group alone — the single largest attrition count across all age groups. This is the organization's most productive and experienced early-career cohort. Losing them at this rate has long-term compounding consequences on institutional knowledge and leadership pipeline.

D. Life Sciences graduates are the most affected by attrition by education field, with 89 exits — nearly 40% of all attritions. Medical background follows at 63. Given that R&D is the highest-attrition department, this pattern is not coincidental — it points toward a specific talent profile (science-educated, R&D-placed, early-to-mid career) that the organization is consistently failing to retain.

E. Gender dynamics reveal an important nuance. Across most age groups, male attrition outpaces female attrition in absolute numbers — most visibly in the 25–34 group (Male 69 vs Female 43). However, in the Under 25 segment the gap narrows significantly (Male 20 vs Female 18), suggesting younger female employees may be at near-equal flight risk — a pattern worth monitoring.

The Story Behind the Numbers
The Opening Picture — 1,470 | 237 | 16% | 1,233 | 37
The dashboard opens with five numbers that together set the scene instantly.
The organization employs 1,470 people in total. Of those, 237 have already left — giving an attrition rate of 16%. That leaves 1,233 active employees carrying the workload today. The average age of 37 tells us this is a mid-career workforce — not a young startup, not a legacy organization. This matters because mid-career employees are expensive to replace and carry institutional knowledge that can't be rehired easily.
The 16% attrition rate is the headline alarm. Industry benchmark for healthy attrition sits between 10–15%. At 16%, this organization has crossed the threshold — and the rest of the dashboard exists to explain why.

Who Is Leaving — The Age Group Story
Under 25: 97 females, 60 males → total workforce in this band: 157
25–34: 554 females, 337 males → largest band in the company
35–44: 505 females, 309 males → second largest
45–54: 245 females, 196 males → workforce starting to thin
Over 55: 132 females, 113 males → smallest active band
The story here is workforce shape. The company is heaviest in the 25–34 and 35–44 bands — these are its most productive years cohorts. This makes attrition in these groups disproportionately damaging, because losing a 28-year-old costs more than losing a 57-year-old who is naturally near exit.

Where It Is Happening — Attrition by Department
R&D: 133 exits (56.12% of all attrition)
Sales: 92 exits (38.82%)
HR: 12 exits (5.06%)
This is the most important chart on the dashboard. Attrition is not evenly spread — it is heavily concentrated in R&D, which accounts for more than half of all exits despite being just one of three departments. Sales is also bleeding significantly at 92. HR at 12 is essentially stable.
The story this tells leadership: this is not a company-wide culture problem. It is a targeted R&D and Sales retention failure. The interventions — whether compensation, career path, workload, or management — need to be department-specific, not blanket.

Who Specifically Is Leaving — Education Field Attrition
Life Sciences: 89
Medical: 63
Marketing: 35
Technical Degree: 32
Other: 11
Human Resources: 7
Now cross this against the department chart. R&D has 133 exits. Life Sciences and Medical together account for 152 attritions — almost exactly matching R&D's count. This is not coincidence. The people leaving R&D are science-educated professionals — Life Sciences and Medical graduates who are being hired, placed in R&D, and then leaving.
This tells HR where to focus retention strategy: it is one specific talent profile driving the majority of exits. Fix R&D retention for science graduates and the 16% attrition rate drops meaningfully.

The Age × Gender Breakdown — The Bottom Row
Under 25: 38 total — Female 18, Male 20
25–34: 112 total — Female 43, Male 69
35–44: 51 total — Female 14, Male 37
45–54: 25 total — Female 9, Male 16
Over 55: 11 total — Female 3, Male 8
The 25–34 band at 112 attritions is the single largest exit group in the entire company. This is the cohort the organization can least afford to lose — high energy, 3–8 years of experience, growing into leadership roles. Losing 112 people from this group is a pipeline problem that compounds over the next decade.
Male attrition consistently outpaces female attrition across all age bands — most sharply in 25–34 (69 vs 43) and 35–44 (37 vs 14). The one exception worth watching is Under 25, where the gap nearly disappears (20 vs 18) — suggesting younger women are at near-equal flight risk, which may warrant early-career retention programs specifically for that group.

The Single Paragraph Summary
This organization is losing people at 16% — above the healthy threshold — and the data tells a precise story: science-educated professionals in their late 20s and early 30s, placed in R&D, are leaving at a disproportionate rate. Sales is a secondary pressure point. HR is fine. The average age of 37 means the company's core workforce is mid-career and valuable. If this attrition pattern continues unchecked for 2–3 years, the organization will find itself with a hollowed-out R&D function and a Sales team that cannot build institutional momentum. The dashboard doesn't just show a problem — it shows exactly where to fix it.
